# Sports Science Knowledge Base for AdaptiveCoach

## Rate of Perceived Exertion (RPE)

RPE is a subjective scale used to quantify the intensity of exercise. The Borg RPE scale runs from 6 to 20, while the modified CR10 scale runs from 0 to 10. In strength and conditioning, the 0–10 RPE scale (also called the Repetitions in Reserve or RIR-based RPE) is most practical:

- **RPE 10**: Maximum effort, could not perform another rep
- **RPE 9**: Could do 1 more rep at most
- **RPE 8**: Could do 2 more reps (moderately hard)
- **RPE 7**: Could do 3 more reps (challenging but controlled)
- **RPE 6**: Could do 4–5 more reps (moderate effort)
- **RPE 5 and below**: Light to very light effort

### Using RPE in Programming
Training blocks should have target RPE ranges per phase. Accumulation phases typically target RPE 6–7, intensification phases target RPE 8–9, and peak/competition phases target RPE 9–10. Staying below RPE 8 on compound movements during early training blocks reduces injury risk and allows sustainable volume increases. Average session RPE above 8.5 across multiple consecutive sessions is a warning sign of excessive fatigue accumulation.

### RPE Autoregulation
RPE autoregulation allows athletes to adjust load on a session-by-session basis based on how they feel. If the target is RPE 8 for squats and the athlete reaches RPE 8 at a lower weight than previous sessions, this signals accumulated fatigue and warrants reducing volume or intensity.

## Progressive Overload Principles

Progressive overload is the gradual increase in stress placed on the body during training to stimulate continued adaptation. The key variables are:

### Volume Progression
Volume (sets × reps × load) is the primary driver of hypertrophy. A conservative volume increase of 5–10% per week is sustainable for most intermediate lifters. Beginners can tolerate higher weekly increases (10–15%). Advanced athletes should limit volume increases to 3–5% per week to avoid rapid fatigue accumulation. 

**Minimum Effective Volume (MEV)**: The minimum sets per muscle group per week needed to maintain adaptations. Approximately 8–10 sets per muscle group per week.

**Maximum Adaptive Volume (MAV)**: The volume at which gains are maximised. Typically 12–20 sets per muscle group per week depending on training age and recovery capacity.

**Maximum Recoverable Volume (MRV)**: The volume beyond which recovery is insufficient and performance degrades. Exceeding MRV consistently leads to overreaching.

### Intensity Progression
Load (percentage of 1RM or RPE-adjusted) increases should follow a planned progression. Common approaches:
- **Linear progression**: Add 2.5–5kg per session (beginners)
- **Double progression**: Increase reps before increasing weight (intermediates)
- **Wave loading**: Alternate between higher and lower intensity weeks

### Frequency Progression
Training each muscle group 2× per week outperforms 1× per week for hypertrophy. For strength, 3–4 sessions per week on major movements is optimal. Increasing frequency beyond this requires careful volume management to avoid overtraining.

## Acute:Chronic Workload Ratio (ACWR)

ACWR is a sports science metric that compares recent training load (acute) to historical training load (chronic). It is one of the most evidence-based tools for injury risk management.

### Calculation
- **Acute load**: Sum of training volume (or session RPE × duration) over the past 7 days
- **Chronic load**: Rolling average of the past 28 days (4-week average)
- **ACWR = Acute Load / Chronic Load**

### Interpretation
- **ACWR < 0.8**: Undertraining / detraining risk. The athlete is doing significantly less than their established baseline, which may indicate insufficient stimulus for adaptation.
- **ACWR 0.8–1.3**: The "sweet spot." This range is associated with low injury risk and optimal adaptation stimulus.
- **ACWR 1.3–1.5**: Caution zone. Training load is increasing faster than the chronic baseline can accommodate. Monitor for fatigue signs and consider brief load reduction.
- **ACWR > 1.5**: High overreach risk. Injury risk increases significantly. Immediate load reduction is strongly recommended. A deload week should be implemented.

### ACWR and Fatigue Flags
When ACWR > 1.5, the training plan must include at least 2 full rest days in the upcoming week and reduce compound lift intensity by at least 20%. When ACWR is in the amber zone (1.3–1.5), maintain current volume but avoid intensity increases. Monitor for performance degradation, elevated resting heart rate, sleep disruption, and mood changes as indicators of systemic fatigue.

## Deload Protocols

A deload is a planned reduction in training stress designed to allow accumulated fatigue to dissipate while maintaining (or consolidating) fitness adaptations. Deloads should be a structured part of any periodised programme, not an ad-hoc response to exhaustion.

### When to Deload
- Every 4–8 weeks as a scheduled strategy (frequency depends on training intensity and volume)
- When ACWR exceeds 1.5
- When average session RPE increases for the same absolute load across 2+ consecutive sessions
- When performance metrics (e.g., 1RM, sprint times) degrade by >5%
- Following a period of illness, travel, high life stress, or poor sleep

### How to Structure a Deload Week
A deload should reduce one or more of: volume, intensity, or frequency. The most effective approach:

**Volume deload** (recommended for most): Reduce total sets per session by 40–50% while maintaining load. This preserves neuromuscular patterns and intensity adaptations.

**Intensity deload**: Reduce load to 50–60% of 1RM while maintaining set/rep counts. Useful for joint recovery.

**Frequency deload**: Cut training days from 4–5 to 2–3. Useful when life stress is the primary fatigue driver.

During a deload, RPE targets should be capped at 6–7. All compound movements should be performed with conservative loads.

### Post-Deload Supercompensation
Following a properly structured deload, athletes typically experience a 3–7% performance improvement in the first 1–2 weeks of resumed full training. This supercompensation window is ideal for testing 1RMs or introducing new training stimuli.

## Exercise Substitution Principles

Exercise substitution allows programme continuity when an athlete cannot perform a movement due to injury, equipment availability, or excessive fatigue in specific movement patterns.

### Substitution Hierarchy
1. **Same movement pattern, reduced load**: Barbell back squat → Goblet squat or safety bar squat
2. **Same primary muscle group, different equipment**: Barbell bench press → Dumbbell bench press (reduces shoulder joint load, increases ROM)
3. **Same movement plane, reduced complexity**: Barbell deadlift → Trap bar deadlift or Romanian deadlift
4. **Unilateral substitution**: Bilateral leg press → Bulgarian split squat (addresses imbalances, reduces axial loading)

### When to Substitute
- Persistent joint pain or discomfort at RPE < 6 on a movement
- ACWR > 1.5 on lower body compound movements: substitute bilateral patterns for unilateral
- Equipment unavailability
- Technical breakdown at target loads (swap to lighter variation to preserve movement quality)

## Muscle Group Recovery Times

Recovery time is the minimum rest required between high-intensity sessions targeting the same muscle group for full neuromuscular recovery.

### Large Compound Movements (48–72 hours minimum)
- Squats, deadlifts, Romanian deadlifts, hip thrusts
- Overhead press, bench press, weighted pull-ups
- These movements produce high mechanical tension and metabolic stress across multiple large muscle groups and require 48–72 hours of recovery before the next high-intensity session.

### Isolation and Accessory Movements (24–48 hours)
- Bicep curls, tricep extensions, lateral raises
- Leg extensions, leg curls, calf raises
- Lighter isolation work recovers faster due to lower absolute loads and more limited muscle damage.

### Practical Application for Weekly Scheduling
- Never schedule two consecutive high-intensity sessions for the same primary muscle group
- Upper/lower splits can train each region 2× per week with adequate recovery (e.g., Monday/Thursday lower, Tuesday/Friday upper)
- Push/pull/legs splits allow 3× per week frequency with appropriate rest days
- When fatigue level is "amber" or "red", extend recovery windows to 72+ hours and insert additional rest days

## Sports Nutrition for Training Adaptation

### Protein Requirements
- **General athletic population**: 1.6–2.2g protein per kg bodyweight per day
- **During caloric deficit**: Increase to 2.2–2.6g/kg to preserve lean mass
- **During caloric surplus (hypertrophy focus)**: 1.8–2.2g/kg is sufficient
- Protein synthesis is maximally stimulated by doses of 0.4g/kg per meal (approximately 30–40g per meal for a 75kg athlete)
- Post-workout protein within 2 hours post-exercise optimises muscle protein synthesis

### Carbohydrate Periodisation
Carbohydrates are the primary fuel for high-intensity training. Carb periodisation matches carbohydrate intake to training demands:

- **High-intensity training days (strength, HIIT)**: 4–6g carbs/kg bodyweight
- **Moderate training days**: 3–4g carbs/kg
- **Rest or low-intensity days**: 1–2g carbs/kg (allows enhanced fat oxidation without compromising recovery)

Pre-workout carbohydrates (30–60 minutes before): 30–60g of fast-digesting carbs improve performance in sessions > 60 minutes. Post-workout carbohydrates replenish glycogen and synergise with protein for muscle repair.

### Caloric Targets
- **Muscle gain (lean bulk)**: Caloric surplus of 200–350 kcal/day above TDEE. Larger surpluses increase fat gain without meaningfully increasing muscle synthesis rates.
- **Fat loss (cut)**: Caloric deficit of 300–500 kcal/day below TDEE. Larger deficits risk muscle loss, hormonal disruption, and performance degradation.
- **Maintenance (recomp)**: TDEE maintenance calories with high protein. Effective for beginners and detrained athletes.

### TDEE Estimation
Total Daily Energy Expenditure (TDEE) = BMR × Activity Multiplier

Activity multipliers:
- Sedentary (desk job, no exercise): 1.2
- Lightly active (1–3 days/week training): 1.375
- Moderately active (3–5 days/week): 1.55
- Very active (6–7 days/week hard training): 1.725
- Extremely active (2× per day or physical labour): 1.9

### Fat Intake
Dietary fat should constitute 20–35% of total calories. Minimum fat intake of 0.5–1.0g/kg/day is required to maintain hormonal health (testosterone, oestrogen production is fat-dependent). Excessive fat reduction below 20% of calories risks hormonal disruption and impaired recovery.

## Sleep and Recovery

Sleep is the primary anabolic window. Growth hormone secretion is highest during slow-wave sleep (stages 3–4). Inadequate sleep directly impairs muscle protein synthesis, elevates cortisol, and reduces cognitive performance.

### Sleep Requirements for Athletes
- 8–10 hours per night is optimal for athletes in heavy training
- Less than 6 hours of sleep increases injury risk by 1.7× and significantly impairs next-day performance
- Sleep debt is cumulative — a single night of poor sleep is recoverable; chronic sleep restriction is not

### Practical Sleep Strategies
- Maintain consistent sleep/wake times (even on weekends)
- Avoid high-intensity training within 3 hours of bedtime (elevates core temperature and cortisol)
- Casein protein before bed can sustain overnight muscle protein synthesis
- Magnesium supplementation (200–400mg glycinate) can improve sleep quality in athletes

### Sleep and Training Load Interactions
When sleep quality is consistently poor (< 6 hours for 3+ consecutive nights), training load should be reduced by 20–30% regardless of ACWR values, as the physiological recovery capacity is compromised. The AdaptiveCoach system should treat poor sleep reports as a fatigue signal equivalent to amber ACWR status.

## Periodisation Models

### Linear Periodisation
Training progresses from high-volume/low-intensity in early phases to low-volume/high-intensity near competition or testing periods. Simple and effective for beginners. Phases typically last 4–8 weeks.

### Undulating Periodisation
Training variables (volume, intensity) vary within a week rather than across weeks. Daily undulating periodisation (DUP) alternates between hypertrophy (higher rep), strength (moderate rep), and power (lower rep) sessions within the same week. More effective than linear periodisation for intermediate to advanced athletes.

### Block Periodisation
Distinct training blocks each emphasise a specific quality: accumulation (volume), transmutation (intensity), and realisation (peaking). 3–6 week blocks allow targeted adaptations without excessive fatigue accumulation.

### Conjugate/Concurrent Periodisation
Multiple qualities trained simultaneously. Requires careful volume management to avoid interference effects. Works well when strength and conditioning goals must coexist.
